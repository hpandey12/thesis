\subsubsection{Rayleigh-Taylor Instability}
When two superposed fluids with differing densities are accelerated in a direction perpendicular to their interface, the surface is unstable when the heavier fluid is accelerated into the lighter fluid.~\citelater{ref 15, p192 - Birkhoff, 1954}. The instability arising therefrom is known as the Rayleigh-Taylor (RT) instability. 
The growth of perturbations at the interface is characterised by opposing pressure and density gradients~\citelater{Banerjee 2020, Schilling et all 2020, HutchinsonDissertation} and the density difference is represented by the \textit{Atwood number}, $At = (\rho_{H} - \rho_{L})/(\rho_{H} + \rho_{L}).$
Although not encountered in its ideal form, the RT instability may occur across various natural and technological processes where it is often modified by the presence of additional processes~\COMMENT{mention which processes?}. The principles governing the nonlinear RT instability also govern any motion of one fluid in another of different density~\citelater{Tryggvason rt instability}, thus serving as a good measure to study the infiltration of the evaporation chamber in the first subproblem~\COMMENT{does it even count? If we have air at 30000 Pa, can it be said it is enough fluid for this to count}. The instability has moreover served as a classical benchmark for the evaluation of the behaviour of two phase flows~\citelater{Goulding et al.}~COMMENT{WHY imminent}.
The instability is simulated on a 2D rectangular domain, $[0, L_{x}] \cross [0, 4L_{x}]$, where $L_{x}$ is the reference length. The initial interface, located at $y_{0}$, represents a single-mode perturbation with amplitude, $A = 0.1 L_{x}$, and wavelength, $\lambda = L_{x}$.
        \begin{equation*}
            y_{0} = 2 L_{x}+ A \cos(\frac{2\pi}{ L_{x} })
        \end{equation*}
        
The lower limit of the high Atwood number regime requires the Atwood number to be atleast one half~\cite{HutchinsonDISSERTATIONSUBMITTEDFACULTY}. Gravitational acceleration acts in a downward direction with a magnitude of $g = 1.0$. Periodic boundary conditions are enforced in the horizontal direction and slip wall conditions are applied at the top and bottom boundaries. The physical parameters are selected to make the simulations dynamically similar to the ones studied~\cite{hamzehlooDirectNumericalSimulations2021}.
        
According to~\cite{MIRJALILI2019221}, a reasonable solution to the RT instability with $At \approx 0.75$ can be obtained with a $128 \cross 512$ mesh. With similar mesh resolutions being used in~\cite{hamzehlooDirectNumericalSimulations2021, HuangConsistentEssentiallyConservative2020} to study similar RT instabilities, in addition to a mesh convergence study being performed, the domain is discretised using a uniform trimmer mesh with size $\delta_{h} = 0.0045$. The timestep for the simulations is selected as $\delta t = 2.5 \cross 10e-4/\sqrt{At}$ similar to~\cite{DingDiffuseInterfaceModel2007,hamzehlooDirectNumericalSimulations2021}. In a system of two fluids with a perturbed interface, the heavier fluid penetrates the lighter fluid due to gravitation thereby pushing the lighter fluid upwards. The initial perturbations, generally studied using linear stability analysis, lead to an exponential growth~\citelater{andrews and dalziel} and transition into non-lineartiy resulting in the rise and sink of the bubbles of lighter fluid and spikes of the heavier fluid respectively~\citelater{sharp, bannerjee}. Figure~\ref{rt_definitions} illustrates the parameter definitions and nomenclature of an evolving single-mode~\COMMENT{need i define single mode rt?} RT instability interface. The evolution of RT instabilities for Reynolds numbers 300, 1000, and 3000 are depicted in figure~\ref{rt_reynolds}. As the solution evolves, the penetration of the spike increases and it eventually rolls up and forms two transient counter-rotating vortices due to shear forces and Kelvin-Helmholtz type instabilities~\citelater{hamzelhoo}, which go on to contribute to turbulent mixing and chaotic flow features~\citelater{Sharp}.
