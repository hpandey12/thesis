\subsection{Stefan Problem}
Phase change processes are characterized by the movement of an interface separating two
 distinct phases, where one phase expands as another contracts due to thermal or non-thermal 
 mechanisms~\cite{Koga2020}. The mathematical model governing such moving boundary 
 problems is known as the Stefan system~\cite{Koga2020}, and the analysis of such a 
 system forms the basis of the Stefan problem.

In the classical Stefan problem, a fixed-temperature boundary propagates a melting or freezing
 front into the domain, with interface movement determined by the balance of latent and conducted 
 heat.~\cite{jonssonOneDimensionalStefan} With uniform initial temperature, constant boundary temperature 
 and thermophysical properties in each phase, the semi-infinite 1D Stefan problem is the simplest, 
 explicitly solvable problem of phase change. As the problem admits an analytical solution and 
 reduces to the standard transient heat conduction problem when latent heat is neglected, it 
 serves as a rigorous benchmark for validating phase-change modelling and the correct implementation of 
 the energy equation.~\cite{jonssonOneDimensionalStefan, bolediLevelsetBasedSpacetime2022, andreucciLectureNotesStefan}.


Assuming that the region in space, $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^2$, occupied by the material is the 2D semi-infinite rectangle 
 $\Omega = \{(x, y) \in \mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R} : x \in [0, \infty), y \in [0, \Delta h]\}$,
 with the solid phase and the liquid phase occupying the regions $\Omega_s$ and $\Omega_l$ respectively 
 such that $\Omega = \Omega_s \cup \Omega_l$. At any given time, $t$, the two phases are separated by 
 the interface, $X(t)$, that marks the change in phase of the material. The boundary of the domain $\Omega$ is denoted by $\partial \Omega$.

The problem space, $\mathcal{D} = \{[t_0, t_f] \times \Omega\}$, is the spatial and temporal domain occupied 
 by the material. With the heat equation governing the temperature field in the liquid region, the Stefan condition 
 at the interface, and the respective boundary conditions, the system of equations defining the one-dimensional Stefan problem can be written as,

\begin{subequations}\label{eq:stefan-equation-system}
\begin{align}
\frac{\partial T}{\partial t} = \alpha\,\frac{\partial^2 T}{\partial x^2}&,\ \text{for $0 < x < X(t), t_0 < t < t_f$}\\
-k \frac{\partial T(X(t), t)}{\partial x}\Bigr|_{X^-(t)} = \rho L \frac{\partial X(t)}{\partial t}&,\ \text{for $t_0 < t < t_f$}\\
T(x, 0) = T_m&,\ \text{on $\Omega$}\\
T(0, t) = T_l&,\ \text{on $\partial\Omega(0, t)$ for $t_0 < t < t_f$}\\
\nabla T \cdot \vetor{n} = 0&,\ \text{on $\partial\Omega - \partial\Omega(0, t)$}
\end{align}
\end{subequations}

An analytical solution to the system of equations is given as
\begin{align}
\hat{T}(x, t) &= T_l - (T_l - T_m)\frac{\text{erf}\left(\frac{x}{2\sqrt{\alpha t}}\right)}{\text{erf}\left(\frac{X(t)}{2\sqrt{\alpha t}}\right)},\\
\hat{X}(t) &= 2\lambda\sqrt{\alpha t},
\end{align}

where $\lambda$ is the unique root of the monotonic function
$$f(\lambda) := \frac{C_p(T_m - T_l)}{L} - \frac{\lambda e^{-\lambda^2}}{\sqrt{\pi}\text{erf}(\lambda)}$$

The error function $\text{erf}(x)$ is defined as $\text{erf}(x) := 2/\sqrt{\pi}\int_0^x e^{-y^2}\,dy$. The physical parameters and computational methodology are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:stefan-simulation-params}

The simulations assume a laminar but unsteady flow. The computational domain of the simulation is chosen 
 to be $[0, L] \times [0, 4\Delta h]$, where $\Delta h$ is the cell width of the discretised domain. The 
 domain is discretised using a trimmed cell mesher~\COMMENT{CONFIRM} with uniform quadrilateral elements. The volume of 
 fluid method with first order HRIC is used to reconstruct the interface. The convection and diffusion terms are 
 discretised using the second-order upwind scheme and the least squares method was used to interpolate the values of 
 the gradients from the cell centres onto the face centres. The transient term is discretised using the implicit 
 Euler-method which is first-order accurate. All four boundaries are assigned a no-slip shear stress specification. 
 The boundary at $x = 0$ is assigned a constant temperature, $T_l = 300$\,K, while the other boundaries are assumed 
 to be adiabatic. Since Star-CCM+ sets under- and over-relaxation factors conservatively, the values are left unchanged.

\subsection{Rayleigh-Taylor Instability}
The Rayleigh-Taylor (RT) instability occurs when a denser fluid is accelerated into a lighter one, leading to
 interfacial perturbations and complex vortex formation~\cite{birkhoffTAYLORINSTABILITYLAMINAR1954}. This 
 phenomenon, known as the Rayleigh-Taylor (RT) instability, is fundamental to many natural and engineering systems. 
 Although the ideal, undisturbed RT instability is rarely encountered in practice, its essential features arise across 
 a broad range of spatial and temporal scales - where the basic mechanism can be influenced by viscosity, surface tension, 
 compressibility, external accelerations, or stratification\cite{SHARP19843}

Perturbation growth is driven by oppositely directed gradients of pressure and density at the interface. The density 
 contrast between the two fluids is represented by the non-dimensional \textit{Atwood number}, 
 $At = (\rho_{H} - \rho_{L})/(\rho_{H} + \rho_{L})$, where $\rho_{H}$ and $\rho_{L}$ denote the 
 densities of the upper (heavy) and lower (light) fluids respectively. The Atwood number quantifies the 
 strength of the instability, with larger values corresponding to greater density contrasts.

The nonlinear development of the RT instability is central to a wide class of interfacial flows where
 one fluid penetrates or displaces another~\cite{tryggvasonNumericalSimulationsRayleighTaylor1988}. 
 Accurately simulating RT instability serves as a classical benchmark for two-phase flow solvers, requiring 
 detailed computation of interface dynamics and rigorous implementation of interphase interaction terms as
 discussed in~\citelater{chapter stuf}, and is widely used to validate numerical methods for multiphase 
 flows~\cite{gouldingSimplifiedConservativeDiscretization2024}.

Considering a region, $\Omega \in \mathbb{R}^2$, where two immiscible, viscous, and incompressible 
 fluids occupy the regions $\Omega_H$ and $\Omega_L$, such that $\Omega = \Omega_H \cup \Omega_L$. 
 The regions $\Omega_{H,L}$ are defined using a scalar height function $h(x, t)$ that graphs the vertical 
 position of the interface at each point along the $x$-axis.

\begin{equation*}
\Omega_i(t) = \{(x, y) \in \mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R} : (-1)^i(y - h(x, t)) > 0, t \geq 0\}, \,\, i = H,L
\end{equation*}

The interface, $X(t)$, separating the two immiscible fluids is given as

\begin{equation*}
X(t) = \{(x,y) \in \mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R}: y = h(x,t),\, t \geq 0 \}.
\end{equation*}

With the initial position of the interface denoted by $X(t=t_0) = X_0$, and the initial velocity field 
 by $\vetor{v}(t=t_0) = \vetor{v}_{0}$, the flow is governed by the incompressible Navier-Stokes equations, 
 surface tension $\sigma$, and acceleration $\vetor{g}$. The jump conditions at the interface preserve the velocity 
 field and relate the normal stresses at the interface to the mean curvature.

If density $\rho$ can be assumed to be $\rho_{H,L}$ or the mixture density in cases of the two-fluid and VOF methods 
respectively. The same can be extended to the velocity, $\vetor{v}$ and the dynamic viscosity $\mu$.

The system of equations governing this two-phase flow system can be expressed as:
\begin{alignat}{2}\label{eq:rt-equation-system}
\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \rho \vetor{v} &= 0 &\quad &\text{in $\Omega_{i}(t)$}\\
\frac{\partial (\rho \vetor{v})}{\partial t} + \nabla(\rho \vetor{v} \otimes \vetor{v}) &= -\nabla p + \mu \nabla^{2} \vetor{v} + \rho \vetor{g}&\quad &\text{in $\Omega_{i}(t)$}\\
\nabla \cdot \vetor{v} &= 0 \\
[[\vetor{v}]] &= 0\\
[[\left(\mu(\nabla\vetor{v} + (\nabla\vetor{v})^{T}) - (p+\rho\vetor{g})\tens{I}\right)\vetor{n}]] &= \sigma \kappa \vetor{n} + [[\rho]]\vetor{g}\\
\vetor{v}(\vetor{x},t_0) &= \vetor{v}_{0}\\
X(t_0) &= X_{0}
\end{alignat}

where $\vetor{n}$ is the normal vector to the interface, $p$ is the pressure field, $\tens{I}$ is the second-order 
 identity tensor, and $\kappa$ in this case denotes the mean curvature of the interface.

In order to simulate a single mode perturbance to the interface, the initial position of the interface is defined using 
 the amplitude, $A = 0.1 L_{x}$, and wavelength, $\lambda = L_{x}$ as follows

\begin{equation*}
X_{0} = 2 L_{x}+ A \cos\left(\frac{2\pi x}{ L_{x} }\right)
\end{equation*}

The dimension $L_{x}$ is the characteristic length which is chosen to be the width of the simulated domain.

The numerical simulations assume turbulent and unsteady flow. The computational domain is a 
 two-dimensional rectangle, $[0, L_x] \times [0, 4L_x]$ that is discretised using uniform orthogonal quadrilateral cells. 
 Convective and diffusive terms are discretized by the second-order upwind scheme; cell-center gradients are interpolated 
 to face centers using the least-squares method. Interface capturing is performed with the volume-of-fluid (VOF) method 
 using the first-order HRIC scheme. The transient term is approximated using the first-order implicit Euler method. Turbulence 
 effects are modeled using the Shear Stress Transport (SST) $k$-$\omega$ [better word?] closure, and the surface tension force 
 is treated semi-implicitly. Gravitational acceleration acts downward, $g = 1.0$\,m/s$^2$. Periodic boundary conditions are imposed 
 in the horizontal direction and slip walls at the top and bottom.

The DNS study undertaken in~\cite{hamzehlooDirectNumericalSimulations2021} is taken to be the canonical benchmark and the 
 physical parameters are chosen to maintain dynamic similarity.

According to~\cite{MIRJALILI2019221}, a reasonable solution to the RT instability with $At \approx 0.75$ can be obtained 
 with a $128 \times 512$ mesh. With similar mesh resolutions being used 
 in~\cite{hamzehlooDirectNumericalSimulations2021, HuangConsistentEssentiallyConservative2020} to study similar RT 
 instabilities, the domain is discretised using a uniform orthogonal quadrilateral mesh with 
 size $\Delta h = 4.5 \times 10^{-3}$\,m. Additional mesh sizes are evaluated in order to study 
 mesh convergence. Time is non-dimensionalised using the Atwood number and the timestep for the 
 simulations, similar to~\cite{DingDiffuseInterfaceModel2007,hamzehlooDirectNumericalSimulations2021}, 
 is selected as $\Delta t = 2.5 \times 10^{-4}/\sqrt{At}$\,s.

\subsection{Liquid Infiltration of Evaporation Chamber}
The evaporation chamber and the nozzle are modelled together as a 2D domain as shown in 
 Figure~\COMMENT{FIG}. Star-CCM+, during the creation of a 2D domain, prescribes an automatic, 
 unchangeable depth of $1$\,m to the model. In order to maintain dynamic similarity, 
 the mass flow rate is compared between the 3D experiment and the 2D model. To this end, 
 the inlet velocity is kept similar in the two experiments. It is assumed that approximately $150$\,mL water is 
 discharged into the evaporation chamber during the experiment.
 
The diameter of the inlet, $d_{in} = 0.017m$, 
 is taken to be the reference length. Assuming a discharge time $t_{dis} = 10$\,s and a constant flow rate,
 the inflow velocity of water in
 the experiment can be calculated as
 \begin{equation}
    \dot{Q} = Q/t_{dis} = 0.00015/10 = v \left(\pi d_{in}^{2}/4\right)
 \end{equation}
The flow rate is assumed to be constant for the discharge time. The inlet velocity is thus dependent on the
 discharge time as $v = 0.00015/\left(d_{in}\,t_{dis}\right)$. The gravitational acceleration is assumed to 
 be $\vetor{g} = 0.4$\,m$\cdot$s$^{-2}$ along the negative $y$-axis.

In the actual 3D experiment, the height of the water at the end of the injection under steady state conditions
 can be estimated using the dimensions and the volume of the discharged water to $\approx 42$\,mm. The 
 water injection is similarly cutoff, at around $2.75$\,s, after the water level reaches approximately $42$\,mm in the simulation, 
 following which the simulation proceeds to run for another $5$\,s with no inflow velocity.


Section probes present along the mid-section of the evaporation 
 chamber and at various points along the nozzle measure the temperature as they would be measured 
 in the actual experiment. An isosurface defines the water-air interface where the volume fraction 
 of water is $0.5$. This isosurface marks the highest position of the water along the mid-section probe and
 records the $y-$axis velocity at the point. 

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Spatial and Temporal Convergence: }
    In order to assess the spatial convergence of the solution, four uniform quadrilateral meshes are used. Using the reference length,
    $L_{x}$, the mesh sizes from coarse to fine are $\Delta \text{h}_{1} = d_{in}/4$, $\Delta \text{h}_{2} = d_{in}/8$, $\Delta \text{h}_{3} = d_{in}/16$,
     and $\Delta \text{h}_{4} = d_{in}/32$ respectively. A uniform timestep,
    $\Delta t = 0.001$\,s is used for all the meshes along with the same initial conditions. Data is recorded at every $0.01$\,s.
    The maximum height of the isosurface against the middle section is used to measure the water level. The velocities at 
    various points along the middle section of the chamber are also recorded. Figure~\ref{fig:sub1_conv} depicts the
    recorded water level against simulation time and the y-axis component of the velocities 
    measured along the middle section at the simulation stop time, $t_{f}$. All quantities have been non-dimensionalised
    using the reference length, velocity, and time scales.

    The coarsest mesh size, h$_{1}$, tends to behave like a vibrating membrane with no discernible development of any waves. The fluid bulk does 
    not contain any air pockets as the mesh is too coarse to resolve any fine details. The finer mesh, h$_{2}$, is able to resolve
    bubbles in the fluid bulk during times of low bubble velocities and when the bubble diameter spans atleast four cells.
    The coarse mesh is, however, able to resolve waves and the oscillatory motion of the isosurface is clearly noticeable.
    The finest mesh size h$_{4}$ is able to resolve merging and breaking apart 
    of the bubbles, droplets escaping the bulk fluid, as well as the flow features developed due to the interaction between the phases at the 
    free surface. The mesh h$_{3}$ while also resolving the bubble interactions in the bulk flow and some coarse droplets, is unable to resolve
    the interface as sharply. However, the increasing void fraction is apparent with decreasing mesh size.
