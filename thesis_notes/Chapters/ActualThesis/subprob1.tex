The flow system assembly comprises of the hydraulic accumulator, the evaporation chamber and the injection system subassembly

The over-pressure in the hydraulic accumulator that drives the water and the absolute pressure in the evaporation chamber are assumed
 to be $3.5 bar$ and $1 bar$ respectively. Water injection stops shortly before the apogee is reached and the flow is cut-off when the
 absolute pressure in the evaporation chamber rises to $1.7 bar$. As no flow measurements are made in the injection assembly, and the

 the evaporation chamber.
 Using the pressure gradient between the accumulator and the evaporation chamber, the Darcy-Weisbach equation to estimate the

\subsection{Validation Problems}
\subsubsection{Stefan Problem}
Phase change processes are characterized by the movement of an 
 interface separating two distinct phases, where one phase expands as
 another contracts due to thermal or non-thermal mechanisms~\citelater{Koga, 2013}.
 The mathematical model governing such moving boundary problems is known as the
 Stefan system~\citelater{Koga, 2013}, and the analysis of such a system forms the basis 
 of the Stefan problem.

In the classical Stefan problem, a fixed-temperature boundary propagates a melting or freezing front into the domain,
 with interface movement determined by the balance of latent and conducted heat.~\citelater{jonas, jonsson}
 With uniform initial temperature, constant boundary temperature and 
 thermophysical properties in each phase, the semi-infinite 1D Stefan problem is the simplest, explicitly solvable problem of phase 
 change. As the problem admits an analytical solution and reduces to the standard
 transient heat conduction problem when latent heat is neglected,
 it serves as a rigorous benchmark for validating phase-change modelling
 and the correct implementation of the energy equation.~\citelater{lectures, uni tenessee knoxville lectures,jonsson 2017}.

Assuming that the region in space, $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^{2}$, occupied by the material is the 2D semi-infinite rectangle 
$\Omega = \{(x,y) \in \mathbb{R} \cross \mathbb{R}:\,x \in [0, \infty),\,y \in [0, \Delta h]\}$, with the solid phase and the liquid phase 
occupying the regions $\Omega_{s}$ and $\Omega_{l}$ respectively such that $\Omega = \Omega_{s} \cup \Omega_{l}$. At any given time, $t$, the 
two phases are separated by the interface, $\Gamma(t)$, that marks the change in phase of the material. The boundary of the domain $\Omega$ is 
denoted by $\partial \Omega$.
The problem space, $\mathcal{D}$, is the spatial and temporal domain occupied by the material, $\mathcal{D} = \{[t_{0}, t_{f}] \cross \Omega\}$. 
With the heat equation governing the temperature field in the liquid region, the Stefan condition at the interface, and the respective boundary 
conditions, the system of equations defining the one-dimensional Stefan problem can be written as,
\begin{align}
  \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} &= \alpha\,\frac{\partial^2 T}{\partial x^2} \quad &0 < x < s(t), \\
  T(x,0) &= T_\mathrm{init}, \\
  T(0,t) &= T_\mathrm{b}, \\
  -k \frac{\partial T}{\partial x}\Bigr|_{x=s(t)^-} &= \rho L \frac{ds}{dt},\\
  T(s(t), t) &= T_\mathrm{m}.
\end{align}
The simulation assumes laminar and unsteady flow and solves for the solution variables implicitly using the SIMPLE scheme.
 The 2D region is discretised using a uniform orthogonal quadrilateral mesh of varying sizes in order to 
 study the spatial convergence of the solution. The physical parameters for the simulation are selected according to water ice, as in~\citelater{boledi et al}.
 A constant density $\rho = 1000\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^{-3}$, dynamic viscosity
 $\mu = 8.8871e-4\,\text{Pa}\cdot\text{s}$, latent heat of fusion $100000 \,\text{J}\cdot\text{kg}^{-1}$, and 
 specific heat $4200\,\text{J}\cdot\text{kg}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$. Thermal conductivities are defined as $2.33\,\text{W}\cdot\text{m}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$ 
 and $0.62\,\text{W}\cdot\text{m}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$ for the liquid and solid phases respectively. 
 All four boundaries are assigned a no-slip shear stress specification. The boundary at $x = 0$ is assigned a constant temperature, $T =273\,\text{K}$, 
 while the other boundaries are assumed to be adiabatic. 
 The convection and diffusion
 terms are discretised using the second-order upwind scheme and the least squares method was used to interpolate the values of the 
 gradients from the cell centres onto the face centres. The transient term is discretised using the implicit Euler-method which is first-order accurate.
 First-order HRIC is used to reconstruct the interface. Since Star-CCM+ sets under- and over-relaxation factors conservatively, the values are left unchanged.

 is observed to move towards the right with increasing simulation time. The temperatue of the system can also be seen to decrease from

\subsubsection{Rayleigh-Taylor Instability}
The Rayleigh-Taylor (RT) instability occurs when a denser fluid is
 accelerated into a lighter one, leading to interfacial perturbations and
 complex vortex formation~\cite{birkhoffTAYLORINSTABILITYLAMINAR1954}.
 This phenomenon, known as the Rayleigh-Taylor (RT) instability, is fundamental
 to many natural and engineering systems. Although the ideal, undisturbed RT instability
 is rarely encountered in practice, its essential features arise across a broad range
 of spatial and temporal scales—where the basic mechanism can be influenced by viscosity,
 surface tension, compressibility, external accelerations, or stratification\cite{SHARP19843}
 Perturbation growth is driven by oppositely directed gradients of pressure and density at the interface.

The density contrast between the two fluids is represented by the non-dimensional
 \textit{Atwood number}, $At = (\rho_{H} - \rho_{L})/(\rho_{H} + \rho_{L})$,
 where $\rho_{H}$ and $\rho_{L}$ denote the densities of the upper (heavy) and lower (light) fluids, 
 respectively. The Atwood number quantifies 
 the strength of the instability, with larger values corresponding to greater density contrasts.
 The nonlinear development of the RT instability is central to a wide class of interfacial flows
 where one fluid penetrates or displaces
 another~\cite{tryggvasonNumericalSimulationsRayleighTaylor1988}. Accurately simulating RT instability serves
 as a classical benchmark for two-phase flow solvers, requiring detailed computation of
 interface dynamics and rigorous implementation of interphase interaction terms as discussed
 in~\citelater{chapter stuf}, and is widely used to validate numerical methods for
 multiphase flows~\cite{gouldingSimplifiedConservativeDiscretization2024}.

We assume that the two immiscible, viscuous, and incompressible fluids occupy the regions,
\begin{equation*}
    \Omega_{i}(t) = \{(x,y) \in \mathbb{R} \cross \mathbb{R}: (-1)^{i}(y - h(t,x)) > 0,\,t \geq 0\}, \,\, i = 1,2
\end{equation*}
and are separated by the interface, $\Gamma(t)$ where
\begin{equation*}
    \Gamma(t) = \{(x,y) \in \mathbb{R} \cross \mathbb{R}: y = h(t,x),\, t \geq 0 \}
\end{equation*}
The flow is governed by the incompressible Navier-Stokes equations with
variable density and viscosity fields according to the local phase distribution.
Interface dynamics are subject to jump conditions expressed as
\begin{align}\label{rt: eqn_system}
    \begin{split}
    \begin{cases}
        \rho \frac{\partial (\mathbf{u})}{\partial t} + \rho \nabla \cdot (\mathbf{u} \mathbf{u})& = -\nabla p + \mu \nabla^{2} \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{g}\rho,\\
        \nabla \cdot \mathbf{u}& = 0,\\
        [\![S(u, p + \mathbf{g}\rho)\mathbf{n}]\!] &= \sigma \kappa \mathbf{n} + [\![\rho]\!] \mathbf{g} y\\
        [\![u]\!] &= 0\\
        V &= \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{n}\\
        u(0) &= u_{0},\\
        \Gamma(0) &= \Gamma_{0}
    \end{cases}
\end{split}
\end{align}   
where $\rho$ is the density, $\mathbf{u}$ is the velocity vector, $p$ is the pressure, $\mu$ is the dynamic viscosity, and $g$ is the gravitational acceleration.

The numerical simulations assume turbulent, unsteady flow and are performed using 
 the SIMPLE implicit algorithm.
 The computational domain is a two-dimensional rectangle, $[0, L_x] \times [0, 4L_x]$,
 uniformly discretized by orthogonal quadrilateral cells.
 Convective and diffusive terms are discretized by the second-order upwind scheme; 
 cell-center gradients are interpolated to face centers using the least-squares method. 
 Interface capturing is performed with the volume-of-fluid (VOF) method using the 
 first-order HRIC scheme. The transient term is approximated using the first-order 
 implicit Euler method. 
 Turbulence effects are modeled using the Shear Stress Transport (SST) $k$-$\omega$ \COMMENT{better word?}
 closure, 
 and the surface tension force is treated semi-implicitly. Initial conditions impose a 
 single-mode interface perturbation, with amplitude, $A = 0.1 L_{x}$, and 
 wavelength, $\lambda = L_{x}$.
        \begin{equation*}
           \Gamma_{0} = 2 L_{x}+ A \cos(\frac{2\pi}{ L_{x} })
        \end{equation*}
       
Gravitational acceleration acts downward, $g = 1.0$. 
 Periodic boundary conditions are imposed in the horizontal 
 direction and slip walls at the top and bottom. The Atwood number is varied, 
 with $At \geq 0.5$ denoting the "high Atwood" regime~\cite{HutchinsonDISSERTATIONSUBMITTEDFACULTY}, 
 and physical parameters are chosen for dynamic similarity to canonical benchmark 
 cases~\cite{hamzehlooDirectNumericalSimulations2021}.

According to~\cite{MIRJALILI2019221}, a reasonable solution to the RT instability with $At \approx 0.75$ can be obtained with 
a $128 \cross 512$ mesh. With similar mesh resolutions being used in~\cite{hamzehlooDirectNumericalSimulations2021, 
HuangConsistentEssentiallyConservative2020} to study similar RT instabilities, in addition to a mesh convergence study being performed, 
the domain is discretised using a uniform orthogonal quadrilateral mesh with size $\delta_{h} = 0.0045$. The timestep for the simulations is 
selected as $\delta t = 2.5 \cross 10e-4/\sqrt{At}$ similar to~\cite{DingDiffuseInterfaceModel2007,hamzehlooDirectNumericalSimulations2021}.

 reach finite amplitude, nonlinear effects lead to the formation of rising 
 “bubbles” of the lighter fluid and descending “spikes” of the heavier fluid. 
 Continued evolution is marked by vortex formation and interface roll-up driven 
 by shear and
 Kelvin-Helmholtz type instabilities~\cite{hamzehlooDirectNumericalSimulations2021,tryggvasonNumericalSimulationsRayleighTaylor1988}, 
 which go on to contribute
 to turbulent mixing and chaotic flow features~\cite{SHARP19843}.
