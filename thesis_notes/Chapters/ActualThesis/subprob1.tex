\subsection{Objective}

The objective of the first task is to model the water injection into the evaporation 
 chamber and extract quantities of interest in order to better estimate the initial 
 conditions for the second task. The validity of the multiphase physics models provided 
 by Star-CCM+ and the equations implemented therein is also evaluated against the 
 one-dimensional Stefan problem and the two-dimensional Rayleigh-Taylor instability.

\subsection{Validation Problems}

\subsubsection{Stefan Problem}

Phase change processes are characterized by the movement of an interface separating two
 distinct phases, where one phase expands as another contracts due to thermal or non-thermal 
 mechanisms~\citelater{Koga, 2013}. The mathematical model governing such moving boundary 
 problems is known as the Stefan system~\citelater{Koga, 2013}, and the analysis of such a 
 system forms the basis of the Stefan problem.

In the classical Stefan problem, a fixed-temperature boundary propagates a melting or freezing
 front into the domain, with interface movement determined by the balance of latent and conducted 
 heat.~\cite{jonssonOneDimensionalStefan} With uniform initial temperature, constant boundary temperature 
 and thermophysical properties in each phase, the semi-infinite 1D Stefan problem is the simplest, 
 explicitly solvable problem of phase change. As the problem admits an analytical solution and 
 reduces to the standard transient heat conduction problem when latent heat is neglected, it 
 serves as a rigorous benchmark for validating phase-change modelling and the correct implementation of 
 the energy equation.~\citelater{lectures, uni tenessee knoxville lectures,jonsson 2017}
 \cite{jonssonOneDimensionalStefan, bolediLevelsetBasedSpacetime2022, andreucciLectureNotesStefan}.

Assuming that the region in space, $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^{2}$, occupied by the material is
 the 2D semi-infinite rectangle 
 $\Omega = \{(x,y) \in \mathbb{R} \cross \mathbb{R}:\,x \in [0, \infty),\,y \in [0, \Delta h]\}$, 
 with the solid phase and the liquid phase occupying the regions $\Omega_{s}$ and $\Omega_{l}$ 
 respectively such that $\Omega = \Omega_{s} \cup \Omega_{l}$. At any given time, $t$, the two 
 phases are separated by the interface, $\Gamma(t)$, that marks the change in phase of the material. 
 The boundary of the domain $\Omega$ is denoted by $\partial \Omega$.

The problem space, $\mathcal{D}$, is the spatial and temporal domain occupied by the material,
 $\mathcal{D} = \{[t_{0}, t_{f}] \cross \Omega\}$. With the heat equation governing the 
 temperature field in the liquid region, the Stefan condition at the interface, and the 
 respective boundary conditions, the system of equations defining the one-dimensional Stefan problem can be written as,

\begin{align}
\frac{\partial T}{\partial t} &= \alpha\,\frac{\partial^2 T}{\partial x^2} \quad &0 < x < s(t), \\
T(x,0) &= T_\mathrm{init}, \\
T(0,t) &= T_\mathrm{b}, \\
-k \frac{\partial T}{\partial x}\Bigr|_{x=s(t)^-} &= \rho L \frac{ds}{dt},\\
T(s(t), t) &= T_\mathrm{m}.
\end{align}

The simulation assumes laminar and unsteady flow and solves for the solution variables
 implicitly using the SIMPLE scheme. The 2D region is discretised using a uniform orthogonal quadrilateral 
 mesh of varying sizes in order to study the spatial convergence of the solution. The physical parameters 
 for the simulation are selected according to water ice, as in~\citelater{boledi et al}. A constant density 
 $\rho = 1000\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^{-3}$, dynamic viscosity $\mu = 8.8871e-4\,\text{Pa}\cdot\text{s}$, 
 latent heat of fusion $100000 \,\text{J}\cdot\text{kg}^{-1}$, and specific heat 
 $4200\,\text{J}\cdot\text{kg}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$. Thermal conductivities are defined as 
 $2.33\,\text{W}\cdot\text{m}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$ and $0.62\,\text{W}\cdot\text{m}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$ 
 for the liquid and solid phases respectively.

All four boundaries are assigned a no-slip shear stress specification. The boundary at $x = 0$ is assigned
 a constant temperature, $T =273\,\text{K}$, while the other boundaries are assumed to be adiabatic. 
 The convection and diffusion terms are discretised using the second-order upwind scheme and the least 
 squares method was used to interpolate the values of the gradients from the cell centres onto the face centres. 
 The transient term is discretised using the implicit Euler-method which is first-order accurate. First-order 
 HRIC is used to reconstruct the interface. Since Star-CCM+ sets under- and over-relaxation factors conservatively, 
 the values are left unchanged.

\subsubsection{Rayleigh-Taylor Instability}

The Rayleigh-Taylor (RT) instability occurs when a denser fluid is accelerated into a lighter one, leading to
 interfacial perturbations and complex vortex formation~\cite{birkhoffTAYLORINSTABILITYLAMINAR1954}. This 
 phenomenon, known as the Rayleigh-Taylor (RT) instability, is fundamental to many natural and engineering systems. 
 Although the ideal, undisturbed RT instability is rarely encountered in practice, its essential features arise across 
 a broad range of spatial and temporal scales—where the basic mechanism can be influenced by viscosity, surface tension, 
 compressibility, external accelerations, or stratification\cite{SHARP19843}

Perturbation growth is driven by oppositely directed gradients of pressure and density at the interface. The density 
 contrast between the two fluids is represented by the non-dimensional \textit{Atwood number}, 
 $At = (\rho_{H} - \rho_{L})/(\rho_{H} + \rho_{L})$, where $\rho_{H}$ and $\rho_{L}$ denote the 
 densities of the upper (heavy) and lower (light) fluids, respectively. The Atwood number quantifies the strength of the instability, with larger values corresponding to greater density contrasts.

The nonlinear development of the RT instability is central to a wide class of interfacial flows where
 one fluid penetrates or displaces another~\cite{tryggvasonNumericalSimulationsRayleighTaylor1988}. 
 Accurately simulating RT instability serves as a classical benchmark for two-phase flow solvers, requiring 
 detailed computation of interface dynamics and rigorous implementation of interphase interaction terms as
 discussed in~\citelater{chapter stuf}, and is widely used to validate numerical methods for multiphase 
 flows~\cite{gouldingSimplifiedConservativeDiscretization2024}.

We assume that the two immiscible, viscous, and incompressible fluids occupy the regions,
\begin{equation*}
\Omega_{i}(t) = \{(x,y) \in \mathbb{R} \cross \mathbb{R}: (-1)^{i}(y - h(t,x)) > 0,\,t \geq 0\}, \,\, i = 1,2
\end{equation*}

and are separated by the interface, $\Gamma(t)$ where
\begin{equation*}
\Gamma(t) = \{(x,y) \in \mathbb{R} \cross \mathbb{R}: y = h(t,x),\, t \geq 0 \}
\end{equation*}

The flow is governed by the incompressible Navier-Stokes equations with variable density and viscosity
 fields according to the local phase distribution. Interface dynamics are subject to jump conditions expressed as

\begin{align}\label{rt: eqn_system}
\begin{split}
\begin{cases}
\rho \frac{\partial (\mathbf{u})}{\partial t} + \rho \nabla \cdot (\mathbf{u} \mathbf{u})& = -\nabla p + \mu \nabla^{2} \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{g}\rho,\\
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u}& = 0,\\
[\![S(u, p + \mathbf{g}\rho)\mathbf{n}]\!] &= \sigma \kappa \mathbf{n} + [\![\rho]\!] \mathbf{g} y\\
[\![u]\!] &= 0\\
V &= \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{n}\\
u(0) &= u_{0},\\
\Gamma(0) &= \Gamma_{0}
\end{cases}
\end{split}
\end{align}

where $\rho$ is the density, $\mathbf{u}$ is the velocity vector, $p$ is the pressure, $\mu$ is the dynamic 
 viscosity, and $g$ is the gravitational acceleration.

The numerical simulations assume turbulent, unsteady flow and are performed using the SIMPLE 
 implicit algorithm. The computational domain is a two-dimensional rectangle, $[0, L_x] \times [0, 4L_x]$, 
 uniformly discretized by orthogonal quadrilateral cells. Convective and diffusive terms are discretized by 
 the second-order upwind scheme; cell-center gradients are interpolated to face centers using the least-squares method.

Interface capturing is performed with the volume-of-fluid (VOF) method using the first-order HRIC scheme. 
 The transient term is approximated using the first-order implicit Euler method. Turbulence effects are modeled 
using the Shear Stress Transport (SST) $k$-$\omega$ \COMMENT{better word?} closure, and the surface tension force is 
treated semi-implicitly. Initial conditions impose a single-mode interface perturbation, with amplitude, $A = 0.1 L_{x}$, 
and wavelength, $\lambda = L_{x}$.

\begin{equation*}
\Gamma_{0} = 2 L_{x}+ A \cos(\frac{2\pi}{ L_{x} })
\end{equation*}

Gravitational acceleration acts downward, $g = 1.0$. Periodic boundary conditions are imposed in 
 the horizontal direction and slip walls at the top and bottom. The Atwood number is varied, 
 with $At \geq 0.5$ denoting the "high Atwood" regime~\cite{HutchinsonDISSERTATIONSUBMITTEDFACULTY}, 
 and physical parameters are chosen for dynamic similarity to canonical 
 benchmark cases~\cite{hamzehlooDirectNumericalSimulations2021}.

According to~\cite{MIRJALILI2019221}, a reasonable solution to the RT 
 instability with $At \approx 0.75$ can be obtained with a $128 \cross 512$ mesh. With similar 
 mesh resolutions being used in~\cite{hamzehlooDirectNumericalSimulations2021, HuangConsistentEssentiallyConservative2020} 
 to study similar RT instabilities, in addition to a mesh convergence study being performed, the domain is 
 discretised using a uniform orthogonal quadrilateral mesh with size $\delta_{h} = 0.0045$. The timestep for 
 the simulations is selected as $\delta t = 2.5 \cross 10e-4/\sqrt{At}$ similar 
 to~\cite{DingDiffuseInterfaceModel2007,hamzehlooDirectNumericalSimulations2021}.

Figure~\ref{fig:rt_reynolds} depicts the evolution of the RT instability with varying density ratios
 and Reynolds numbers. The initial perturbations, generally studied using linear stability analysis, 
 experience an exponential growth~\cite{andrewsSmallAtwoodNumber2010}. When perturbations reach finite 
 amplitude, nonlinear effects lead to the formation of rising "bubbles" of the lighter fluid and descending 
 "spikes" of the heavier fluid.

\label{fig:rt_reynolds}
\end{figure}

Continued evolution is marked by vortex formation and interface roll-up driven by shear and Kelvin-Helmholtz 
type instabilities~\cite{hamzehlooDirectNumericalSimulations2021,tryggvasonNumericalSimulationsRayleighTaylor1988}, 
which go on to contribute to turbulent mixing and chaotic flow features~\cite{SHARP19843}.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Effect of Reynolds and Atwood Number:} Increasing the Reynolds number has a relatively small 
 effect on the penetration lengths of the spikes and bubbles - with $Re = 300$ showing the least penetration depths. 
 However, it does have a significant effect on the topology of the interface, with increasingly chaotic vortices visible 
 at the spike rollup and the ensuing turbulent mixing.

High Atwood numbers promote non-linearity and increased turbulent mixing, and affect the initial interface growth. 
 The effect and prominence of the counter-rotating vortices at the tip of the spike decreases and reduces 
 the chaotic flow features at the tips of the secondary vortices. This is readily observed in cases of very 
 high density ratios and simulations of the RT instability with very high density ratios 
 in~\cite{HuangConsistentEssentiallyConservative2020}. The high spike tip velocity results in a 
 negligible spike roll-up and a much simpler interface evolution.

\item \textbf{Grid Convergence:} In order to evaluate the quality of the mesh, the maximum vertical distance of the 
 spike and bubble tip as measured from the undisturbed initial interface, $y^{*} = |y_{s,b} - y_{0}|$, are evaluated 
 for Reynolds numbers, $Re = 300, 3000$, and Atwood number, $At = 0.5$. Locations of the spike and bubble tips are 
 recorded at every time-step and extrapolated in order to compare to the DNS data available 
 from~\cite{hamzehlooDirectNumericalSimulations2021}.
\end{itemize}

Although, Star-CCM+, qualitatively, accurately simulates the RT instability, the calculated 
 RMS and absolute errors in the bubble and spike tip locations leave much to be desired. For
 higher Reynolds numbers, the bubble tip distances are consistently under-predicted for $Re = 1000, 3000$ at
 later simulation times. For $Re = 3000$, at later simulation times, the development of secondary 
 perturbances at the edges of the domain. This results in turbulent structure forming in the bubble 
 region that interfere with the spike and form secondary spikes of their own.

These secondary spikes interfere with the turbulent vortices at the spike tips and the main spike stem. 
 This is, interestingly, not seen for the coarsest mesh size for $Re = 3000$. These secondary 
 perturbations are actually first observed for $Re = 10000$ in~\cite{hamzehlooDirectNumericalSimulations2021}. 
 This can be ascribed to either the lower order scheme used in the Star-CCM+ simulations or the choice of 
 turbulence modelling using eddy viscosity models instead of completely resolving turbulence. For Atwood number 
 that are not close to zero, the Boussinesq approximation is also invalid which is an integral assumption in 
 RANS turbulence models. Star-CCM+, in order to accommodate the spurious currents that may be 
 introduced through the use of eddy viscosity models, provides models that aid in the damping of momentum and/or 
 turbulence at the interface, however, the addition of these models leads to significant deviations from the DNS results.
