\subsection{Problem Description and Background}
In order to validate the continuum hypothesis in the evaporation chamber,
 the Knudsen number is estimated. The Knudsen number, defined as the ratio between the mean free path and
 a scale length of a selected macroscopic flow property. Assuming that the air present in the chamber is dry
 with a molar mass $28.96\,\text{g}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$ and behaves as an
 ideal gas, the mean free path, $\lambda$, can be estimated using the dynamic viscosity, density, and temperature of
 the air in the evaporation chamber.
 Using the remarks provided in the SED, the temperature at start of the experiment inside the evaporation chamber
 can be assumed to be $280\,\text{K}$ in the worst case. Thus using Sutherland's relation and standard sea-level
 conditions for dry air, the dynamic viscosity, $\mu$ results in $1.716e-5 Pa.s$. Consequently, the mean free path,
 \begin{equation}
    \lambda = \frac{\mu}{\rho} \sqrt{\frac{\pi m}{2 k_{B} T}} \approx 2.0732e-06\,\text{m}
 \end{equation}
 The Knudsen number, $K$, is thus of the order $10^{-5}$, confirming the continuum assumption.

\subsubsection{Computational Methodology}
The simulation assumes a turbulent and unsteady flow, utlising the SIMPLE implicit scheme to solve for the solution
 variables. The computational domain is discretised using an orthogonal quadrilateral mesh. Similar to the validation studies,
 the convective and diffusive terms are discretised using the second-order upwind scheme. The least squares method
 is used to estimate the values of the gradients at the face centres and the VOF solver uses the first-order HRIC
 scheme to reconstruct the interface. As the simulation emulates the Rayleigh-Taylor instability at high Atwood numbers,
 the modified K-Omega model is used to model turbulence. The transient term is discretised using the first-order
 implicit Euler-method.

\subsubsection{Stefan Problem}
Phase changes can be regarded as the growth of the domain of one phase accompanied by the reduction of the domain of the other phase. 
This growth or reduction in domain sizes can be triggered by thermal as well as non-thermal processes~\citelater{shumon koga} 
and the representative mathematical dynamic model containing moving boundaries is known as the Stefan system. The associated problem of 
analysing and finding the solutions to the aforementioned model is known as the Stefan problem.~\citelater{koga}. 
The classical Stefan problem is a one dimensional solidification or melting problem~citelater{jonas, jonsson}. The one-dimensional 
Stefan problem under semi-infinite geometry, uniform initial temperature, constant boundary temperature, and 
thermophysical properties constant in each phase is probably the simplest, explicitly solvable problem of phase 
change~\citelater{lectures, uni tenessee knoxville lectures}, serving as a good benchmark to validate the phase change VOF solver in Star-CCM+.
~\COMMENT{DOES IT ALSO SERVE AS A GOOD BENCHMARK FOR SIMPLE HEAT CONDUCTION TOO??? BECOZ TWO BIRDS ONE STONE}

Assuming that the region in space, $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^{2}$, occupied by the material is the 2D semi-infinite rectangle 
$\Omega = \{(x,y) \in \mathbb{R} \cross \mathbb{R}:\,x \in [0, \infty),\,y \in [0, \Delta h]\}$, with the solid phase and the liquid phase 
occupying the regions $\Omega_{s}$ and $\Omega_{l}$ respectively such that $\Omega = \Omega_{s} \cup \Omega_{l}$. At any given time, $t$, the 
two phases are separated by the interface, $\Gamma(t)$, that marks the change in phase of the material. The boundary of the domain $\Omega$ is 
denoted by $\partial \Omega$.
The problem space, $\mathcal{D}$, is the spatial and temporal domain occupied by the material, $\mathcal{D} = \{[t_{0}, t_{f}] \cross \Omega\}$. 
With the heat equation governing the temperature field in the liquid region, the Stefan condition at the interface, and the respective boundary 
conditions, the system of equations defining the one-dimensional Stefan problem can be written as,
\begin{align}\label{stef: eqn_system}
    \begin{split}
    \begin{cases}
        \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} - \alpha \frac{\partial^{2} T}{\partial x^{2}}& = 0,\\
        \rho h_{m} \frac{d \Gamma(x(t), t)}{d t} + \kappa \frac{\partial \Gamma(x(t), t)}{\partial x}& = 0,\\
        T(x, 0)& = T_{l},\\
        \nabla T(x, t) \cdot \mathbf{n}& = 0,\\
        T(x, 0)& = T_{m}
    \end{cases}
\end{split}
\end{align}   
- the system of equations is well-posed and has a unique solution. the evolution of the temperature field along with the evolution of the 
phase change interface is studied.
The simulation assumes laminar and unsteady flow and solves for the solution variables implicitly using the SIMPLE scheme.
 The 2D region is discretised using a uniform orthogonal quadrilateral mesh of varying sizes in order to 
 study the spatial convergence of the solution. The physical parameters for the simulation are selected according to water ice, as in~\citelater{boledi et al}.
 A constant density $\rho = 1000\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^{-3}$, dynamic viscosity
 $\mu = 8.8871e-4\,\text{Pa}\cdot\text{s}$, latent heat of fusion $100000 \,\text{J}\cdot\text{kg}^{-1}$, and 
 specific heat $4200\,\text{J}\cdot\text{kg}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$. Thermal conductivities are defined as $2.33\,\text{W}\cdot\text{m}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$ 
 and $0.62\,\text{W}\cdot\text{m}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$ for the liquid and solid phases respectively. 
 All four boundaries are assigned a no-slip shear stress specification. The boundary at $x = 0$ is assigned a constant temperature, $T =273\,\text{K}$, 
 while the other boundaries are assumed to be adiabatic. 
 The convection and diffusion
 terms are discretised using the second-order upwind scheme and the least squares method was used to interpolate the values of the 
 gradients from the cell centres onto the face centres. The transient term is discretised using the implicit Euler-method which is first-order accurate.
 First-order HRIC is used to reconstruct the interface. Since Star-CCM+ sets under- and over-relaxation factors conservatively, the values are left unchanged.

The evolution of the temperature field for the simulation with cell size, $\Delta h = 5e-3$ is shown in Figure~\COMMENT{FIG}. A clear propagation of
 the interface from the left to right is observed with increasing simulation times. The temperature also gradually decreases in the liquid phase from the Dirichlet
 boundary at $x = 0$ towards the melting temperature, $T_{m}$, at the interface after which it stays constant.

\subsubsection{Rayleigh-Taylor Instability}
When two superposed fluids with differing densities are accelerated in a direction perpendicular to their interface, the surface 
is unstable when the heavier fluid is accelerated into the lighter fluid.~\citelater{ref 15, p192 - Birkhoff, 1954}. The instability 
arising therefrom is known as the Rayleigh-Taylor (RT) instability. 
The growth of perturbations at the interface is characterised by opposing pressure and density 
gradients~\citelater{Banerjee 2020, Schilling et all 2020, HutchinsonDissertation} and the density difference is represented by 
the \textit{Atwood number}, $At = (\rho_{H} - \rho_{L})/(\rho_{H} + \rho_{L}).$
Although not encountered in its ideal form, the RT instability may occur across various natural and technological processes where it 
is often modified by the presence of additional processes~\COMMENT{mention which processes?}. The principles governing the nonlinear 
RT instability also govern any motion of one fluid in another of different density~\citelater{Tryggvason rt instability}, thus 
serving as a good measure to study the infiltration of the evaporation chamber in the first 
subproblem. 
The instability has moreover served as a classical benchmark for the evaluation of the behaviour of two phase 
flows~\citelater{Goulding et al.}~\COMMENT{'WHY' imminent}.

We assume that the two immiscible, viscuous, and incompressible fluids occupy the regions,
\begin{equation*}
    \Omega_{i}(t) = \{(x,y) \in \mathbb{R} \cross \mathbb{R}: (-1)^{i}(y - h(t,x)) > 0,\,t \geq 0\}, \,\, i = 1,2
\end{equation*}
and are separated by the interface, $\Gamma(t)$ where
\begin{equation*}
    \Gamma(t) = \{(x,y) \in \mathbb{R} \cross \mathbb{R}: y = h(t,x),\, t \geq 0 \}
\end{equation*}
Using the navier stokes equations for incompressible fluids, a mixture density and viscosisty, the jump conditions at the interfaces, and the 
initial boundary conditions, the governing equation for the Rayleigh-Taylor instability can be written as
\begin{align}\label{rt: eqn_system}
    \begin{split}
    \begin{cases}
        \rho \frac{\partial (\mathbf{u})}{\partial t} + \rho \nabla \cdot (\mathbf{u} \mathbf{u})& = -\nabla p + \mu \nabla^{2} \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{g}\rho,\\
        \nabla \cdot \mathbf{u}& = 0,\\
        [\![S(u, p + \mathbf{g}\rho)\mathbf{n}]\!] &= \sigma \kappa \mathbf{n} + [\![\rho]\!] \mathbf{g} y\\
        [\![u]\!] &= 0\\
        V &= \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{n}\\
        u(0) &= u_{0},\\
        \Gamma(0) &= \Gamma_{0}
    \end{cases}
\end{split}
\end{align}   
where $\rho$ is the density, $\mathbf{u}$ is the velocity vector, $p$ is the pressure, $\mu$ is the dynamic viscosity, and $g$ is the gravitational acceleration.

In a system of two fluids with a perturbed interface, the heavier fluid penetrates the lighter fluid due to gravitation thereby pushing the lighter fluid upwards.
 The initial perturbations, generally studied using linear stability analysis, lead to an exponential growth~\citelater{andrews and dalziel} and transition into
 non-lineartiy resulting in the rise and sink of the bubbles of lighter fluid and spikes of the heavier fluid respectively~\citelater{sharp, bannerjee}. 
 Figure~\COMMENT{name figure} illustrates the parameter definitions and nomenclature of an evolving single-mode~\COMMENT{need i define single mode rt?} RT 
 instability interface.

The simulation assumer turbulent and unsteady flow, utilising the SIMPLE implicit scheme. The computational domain is discretised using an orthogonal quadrilateral mesh.
 The convection and diffusion terms are discretised using the second-order upwind scheme while the least squares method is again used to interpolate the values
 of the gradients from the cell centres onto the face centres. The VOF solver utilises the first-order HRIC scheme to reconstruct the interface.
 The transient term is discretised using the first-order accurate implicit Euler-method. The modified K-Omega (SST k-omega) model is used to account for turbulence and surface
 is treated semi-implicitly.~\COMMENT{MENTION INITIAL CONDITIONS}
The instability is simulated on a 2D rectangular domain, $[0, L_{x}] \cross [0, 4L_{x}]$, where $L_{x}$ is the reference length. 
 The initial interface, located at $y_{0}$, represents a single-mode perturbation with amplitude, $A = 0.1 L_{x}$, and 
 wavelength, $\lambda = L_{x}$.
        \begin{equation*}
           \Gamma_{0} = 2 L_{x}+ A \cos(\frac{2\pi}{ L_{x} })
        \end{equation*}
       
The lower limit of the high Atwood number regime requires the Atwood number to be atleast $0.5$~\cite{HutchinsonDISSERTATIONSUBMITTEDFACULTY}. 
Gravitational acceleration acts in a downward direction with a magnitude of $g = 1.0$. Periodic boundary conditions are enforced in the 
horizontal direction and slip wall conditions are applied at the top and bottom boundaries. The physical parameters are selected to make 
the simulations dynamically similar to the ones studied in~\cite{hamzehlooDirectNumericalSimulations2021}.
        
According to~\cite{MIRJALILI2019221}, a reasonable solution to the RT instability with $At \approx 0.75$ can be obtained with 
a $128 \cross 512$ mesh. With similar mesh resolutions being used in~\cite{hamzehlooDirectNumericalSimulations2021, 
HuangConsistentEssentiallyConservative2020} to study similar RT instabilities, in addition to a mesh convergence study being performed, 
the domain is discretised using a uniform orthogonal quadrilateral mesh with size $\delta_{h} = 0.0045$. The timestep for the simulations is 
selected as $\delta t = 2.5 \cross 10e-4/\sqrt{At}$ similar to~\cite{DingDiffuseInterfaceModel2007,hamzehlooDirectNumericalSimulations2021}.
