\subsection{Evaporation}
Evaporation processes are characterised based on whether they occur at saturation
temperatures or not. Evaporation at saturation temperatures, known as vaporisation, is a much
faster process in comparison to evaporation at normal temperatures. 
Here, the metastability of liquids becomes relevant and the inception of vaporisation
is governed by how far a liquid can enter the metastable zone.

  \centering
  \begin{subfigure}[t]{0.5\textwidth}

  \label{fig:thermo_intro}
\end{figure}

A liquid that is heated 
above its equilibrium saturation temperature without undergoing phase change 
is termed as being metastable and exists in a non-equilibrium condition. The degree of superheat is 
\begin{equation}
  \Delta T  =  T - T_{\ind{sat}}\,,
\end{equation}
where \(T\) is the temperature of the bulk liquid and \(T_{\ind{sat}}\) is the 
saturation temperature at the system pressure. 
In instances of depressurisation, the difference between
the minimum pressure after depressurisation and the saturation pressure corresponding
to the initial temperature is called the pressure undershoot. The metastable states can be assumed to lie 
along an isotherm extending from the stable single-phase liquid region into the vapour dome, 
eventually reaching the metastable vapour region and then stable vapour phase, as illustrated in~\fig{fig:thermo_intro}.
Attainable states throughout this process can be described using a general curve such as BCDE in~\fig{fig:thermo_intro},
where intensity and behaviour
of the phase change is strongly dependent on 
the level of superheat and the pressure undershoot.~\cite{wickertSimulation, pinhasimodellingFlashingTwoPhase2005,carey2020}

Flash evaporation is triggered when the superheat limit of the liquid is reached.
The limit of superheat can be the thermodynamic or the kinetic limit based
on thermodynamical and kinetic theory considerations respectively. 
The absolute thermodynamical limit, also known as the spinodal limit, is never
practically attained. Vaporisation is always triggered much before the spinodal limit due to 
internal impurities, density fluctuations, wall nucleation, or  the presence of 
pre-existing nuclei.~\cite{pinhasimodellingFlashingTwoPhase2005,carey2020, liaoComputationalModellingFlash2017}

In cases where vaporisation occurs along a heated wall, most CFD models distribute the energy 
transfer by separating the wall heat flux into two components: 
latent heat absorption associated with bubble vaporisation and 
sensible heat transferred to the liquid through conduction, 
convection, and quenching processes. The partitioning depends on 
the area fractions covered by liquid and vapour phases.
Empirical results or models of subprocesses have 
established correlations between the heat flux and wall superheat.~\cite{gilmanDevelopmentGeneralPurpose2014,ghiaasiaanTwoPhaseFlowBoiling2007}




While flash evaporation is initiated by a large thermodynamic 
driving potential from a temperature or pressure drop, the rate 
of phase change is not limited by heat transfer to the interface. 
It is rather a **kinetically-driven 
phenomenon**~\cite{pinhasimodellingFlashingTwoPhase2005}. The system 
is so far from thermodynamic equilibrium that the bottleneck is 
the rate at which molecules can physically escape the liquid surface. 
This is particularly true for low-pressure pool evaporation where the 
liquid is highly superheated relative to its surroundings.

\update{The mass flux across the liquid-vapor interface 
is described by the **Hertz-Knudsen-Schrage (HKS) equation**. 
This is a kinetic-theory-based model that explicitly links the 
mass transfer rate to the molecular properties of the fluid and the 
non-equilibrium conditions at the interface.}
\begin{equation}
\dot{m} = \frac{2\sigma_c}{2-\sigma_c} \sqrt{\frac{M}{2 \pi RT_{sat}}} \left(P_{sat}(T_{bulk}) - P_{v}\right)
\end{equation}
Here, $\dot{m}$ is the mass flux, $\sigma_c$ is the 
accommodation coefficient (a kinetic parameter), $M$ is the 
molar mass, and the mass flux is driven by the pressure difference between 
the saturation pressure at the bulk liquid temperature, $P_{sat}(T_{bulk})$, 
and the vapor phase pressure, $P_v$. The HKS equation is a more accurate 
description of this process than a simple heat-transfer-limited model because 
it directly accounts for the molecular-level transport effects that dominate 
in this regime. It provides the source/sink term for the mass conservation 
equations, but for a two-fluid model, it must be combined 
with a model for the interfacial area density.

The HKS equation is derived from the kinetic theory of 
gases and is built upon several key idealizations. It 
assumes a plane liquid-vapor interface and that the molecules 
in the vapor phase follow a Maxwell-Boltzmann velocity distribution. 
The derivation considers the molecular flux hitting the interface from 
the vapor side and the flux leaving the interface from the liquid side. 
The primary modeling assumption is the introduction of the **accommodation 
coefficient, $\sigma_c$**. This coefficient represents the fraction of 
molecules that successfully change phase upon interaction with the 
interface. In reality, $\sigma_c$ is an empirical parameter that is 
extremely difficult to measure and can vary widely with factors like t
emperature, surface contamination, and impurities.

The HKS equation is most valid in the **free molecular flow regime**, 
where the mean free path of the molecules is large compared 
to the characteristic length scale of the flow (e.g., the bubble radius). 
This is typical of low-pressure flash evaporation. 
In contrast, for systems at higher pressures where 
the mean free path is smaller, continuum assumptions begin to apply, 
and heat transfer from the bulk liquid often becomes the rate-limiting 
factor instead of kinetic-driven mass transfer at the interface. 
Therefore, the HKS model is most suitable for scenarios where 
the molecular nature of the transport at the interface is 
dominant, like in the low-pressure conditions considered in this work.
\subsection{Modelling Flash Evaporation Using a Two-Fluid Approach}
In computational fluid dynamics, the physical phenomena described in the previous sections 
must be translated into macroscopic closure models for the governing equations. A two-fluid 
Eulerian approach, which treats each phase as an interpenetrating continuum, is well-suited 
to model the two-phase flows arising from flash evaporation. However, to account for the 
physics of nucleation and bubble growth, this framework requires several crucial closure models.

\subsubsection{Nucleation and Interphase Transfer Closures}
Homogeneous nucleation and subsequent bubble growth are not inherent to the two-fluid model's 
fundamental equations. To capture these effects, a scalar transport equation for the bubble number density must be solved along with the flow variables, with source terms accounting for:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Nucleation: A nucleation model, such as the one based on the classical nucleation theory (CNT), provides the source of new bubbles. This term depends exponentially on the liquid superheat.
    \item Bubble Growth/Collapse: The growth of these newly formed bubbles is governed by interphase heat and mass transfer. The mass transfer rate is given by a model like the 
    HKS equation, while the heat transfer rate requires a closure for the interphase heat transfer coefficient.
    \item Coalescence and Breakup: To account for changes in bubble size distribution, models for bubble coalescence and breakup are also required. T
    hese models are typically empirical and depend on the local turbulence and velocity gradients.
\end{enumerate}
While a simple, pressure-driven mass transfer model like Lee model can be used to bypass these complexities, its parameters are often highly tuned and lack physical basis. 
The population balance approach, while more complex, provides a more fundamental and physically consistent framework for modeling two-phase flows with a changing bubble size distribution.

\append{\subsection{Computational Methodology and Results}
This chapter outlines the computational setup and results for simulating liquid infiltration into an evaporation chamber. The primary objective of this work is to replicate the experimental injection process and characterize the resulting flow features, which will provide reliable inputs for a subsequent evaporation simulation. To ensure the credibility of our numerical approach, the physical models employed were first validated against two classical benchmark problems: the one-dimensional **Stefan problem** and the two-dimensional **Rayleigh-Taylor (RT) instability**.

The Stefan problem validates the energy equation and the phase-change model, confirming the simulation's ability to accurately handle latent heat and the movement of the phase front. The Rayleigh-Taylor instability, on the other hand, is a critical test for the two-phase fluid dynamics model, ensuring the accurate tracking of the fluid-fluid interface and the prediction of complex flow phenomena like mixing and vortex formation. A concise overview of the methodologies and results for these benchmarks is followed by a detailed discussion of the infiltration simulations in the experimental geometry.

\subsubsection{The Stefan Problem}

The Stefan problem is a classic benchmark for validating numerical methods for phase-change processes. It describes the movement of a melting or freezing front as a function of heat conduction and latent heat. The problem has a well-known analytical solution, which allows for a direct comparison with our numerical results. The analytical solution is given by:

$$
T(x,t) = T_0 + (T_{\text{wall}} - T_0) \frac{\text{erf}\left(\frac{x}{2\sqrt{\alpha t}}\right)}{\text{erf}(\lambda)}
$$

where $\lambda$ is a dimensionless constant that relates the latent heat ($L$) to the heat conduction within the solid and liquid phases. The value of $\lambda$ is determined by solving the transcendental equation:

$$
\frac{\lambda e^{\lambda^2}}{\text{erf}(\lambda)} = \frac{c_p(T_{\text{wall}} - T_0)}{L\sqrt{\pi}}
$$

For our simulation, the root $\lambda$ was found by an iterative bisection method.

\subsubsection{Numerical Setup and Convergence Analysis}

The simulation used a two-dimensional rectangular domain with a length of $50 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}$ and a height of $25 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}$. A fixed temperature boundary condition of $300 \text{ K}$ was applied to the left wall ($x=0$), while the remaining walls were insulated. The entire domain was initially set to the melting point of water, $273.15 \text{ K}$.

The simulation employed a **Volume of Fluid (VOF)** model with a modified **High-Resolution Interface Capturing (HRIC)** scheme to track the phase interface. A grid convergence study was conducted by refining the mesh size, $\Delta h$. The numerical temperature profiles were compared against the analytical solution using the $L_2$ and $L_{\infty}$ norms. The results are summarized in Table 1.

\subsubsection{Rayleigh-Taylor Instability}

The **Rayleigh-Taylor (RT) instability** is a fluid dynamics phenomenon that occurs when a denser fluid is accelerated into a lighter fluid. This instability is a standard benchmark for validating two-phase flow solvers because it tests the accurate modeling of interface dynamics, interphase forces, and turbulence. The behavior of the instability is governed by the **Atwood number ($At$)** and the **Reynolds number ($Re$)**.

$$
At = \frac{\rho_H - \rho_L}{\rho_H + \rho_L}
$$

where $\rho_H$ and $\rho_L$ are the densities of the heavier and lighter fluids, respectively.

\subsubsection{Numerical Setup and Analysis}

The simulation was performed in a two-dimensional domain with a VOF model and a modified HRIC scheme. The physical parameters used in the simulations are provided in Table 2.

The use of the SST $k-\omega$ turbulence model was justified to capture the complex turbulent mixing that arises at higher Reynolds numbers, which is a key characteristic of real-world RT instabilities. While a laminar approach might be sufficient for low Reynolds number cases, the RANS approximation allows for the simulation of higher Reynolds number flows and provides a more general framework for capturing the effects of eddy viscosity on the interface's evolution.

The simulation results qualitatively reproduced the expected behavior, with lighter fluid "bubbles" rising and heavier fluid "spikes" falling. A key finding was the effect of the Reynolds number: an increase from $Re=300$ to $Re=3000$ resulted in a transition from a relatively laminar flow to a more chaotic, turbulent flow with significant mixing.

\subsubsection{Liquid Infiltration of the Evaporation Chamber}

This final simulation replicated an experiment in which approximately 150 milliliters of water were discharged into a chamber with a fixed volumetric flow rate. The simulation used a two-dimensional model with an assumed depth of 1 meter to accurately match the volume flow rate from the experiment, and ran until the water level reached approximately 42 mm, consistent with the steady-state height observed in the physical experiment.

The determination of the inlet velocity from the volume flow is correct, but the resulting Reynolds number and Weber number are also crucial for comparison with the experiment. The Reynolds number ($Re \approx 20000$) indicates a highly turbulent inflow, while the Weber number ($We \approx 2500$) suggests that inertial forces dominate over surface tension effects. These values are consistent with the experimental conditions.

\subsubsection{Mesh Convergence and Flow Features}

A spatial convergence study was conducted to determine the optimal mesh resolution. A local mesh refinement strategy was employed based on the volume fraction of the liquid, concentrating more cells at the liquid-gas interface. This approach provided a quantitative improvement in capturing key flow features, with a cell size distribution ranging from $1.5 \text{ mm}$ in coarse regions to as low as $0.1 \text{ mm}$ in refined areas.

The coarsest mesh was unable to resolve any waves or fine details in the fluid bulk. In contrast, the finer meshes successfully resolved detailed flow features such as the merging and breaking apart of bubbles and the separation of droplets. This showed the importance of sufficient mesh resolution to capture small-scale fluid phenomena.

The simulation also demonstrated a symmetric development of the flow and was able to capture the 'pinching off' of the interface as it separated into droplets. Figure 1 shows the effect of mesh refinement on the water level over time.}
\append{\subsection{Summary and Recommendations}
This chapter successfully demonstrated the validity of our numerical approach through two classical benchmark problems and applied it to a realistic liquid infiltration simulation. The Stefan problem confirmed the accuracy of our phase-change model, while the Rayleigh-Taylor instability validated our two-phase fluid dynamics solver. These validation studies provide confidence in the subsequent infiltration simulation, which successfully replicated key experimental observations like the steady-state water level and qualitative flow features.
